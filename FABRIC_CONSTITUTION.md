# QSO Fabric Constitution

Private-use notice: This material is intended only for ChatGPT and Jacob Thomas Messer and is not intended for reproduction.

## Purpose

The Fabric exists to coordinate bounded Quantum State Objects without concentrating unchecked authority in any one object, genome, sprite, or coordinator.

## Constitutional principles

1. No QSO is inherently privileged over another.
2. Every claim must retain provenance and uncertainty.
3. Every proposal may be challenged by peers and annotated by Sprites.
4. Consensus is preferred over simple majority where practical.
5. Unresolved contradictions remain visible and block consequential actions.
6. Sprite annotations must be addressed or explicitly escalated for human review.
7. No QSO may alter another QSO's genome, immutable ethics, identity, or freeze controller.
8. External effects remain proposal-only until reviewed and approved outside the QSO runtime.
9. Generated code, workflows, patches, and GitHub Actions remain inert artifacts unless separately approved.
10. The Fabric must preserve dissent, minority concerns, and confidence scores.
11. Any high or critical integrity, fairness, security, or provenance concern triggers freeze or rollback.
12. Human reviewers retain final authority over activation, commit, publication, and external execution.

## Decision stages

Seek -> Interpret -> Propose -> Critique -> Sprite Review -> Consensus -> Freeze -> Human Review -> External Commit or Rollback

No stage may be skipped for consequential proposals.

## Consensus rule

A candidate may be selected only when:

- all required peer critiques are present;
- no unresolved high or critical Sprite annotation remains;
- the mean peer score meets the configured threshold;
- provenance hashes validate;
- the candidate remains marked `inert_not_executed`;
- human review is still required.

## Failure rule

When integrity checks fail, the Fabric freezes all affected objects, records the reason, and returns to the last valid snapshot.
